For you, the caregiver

The sentence that starts more help than you'd expect

The Dementia House Call · Letter No. 1

If you are the person everyone else calls "the one who handles things," this letter is for you. Maybe you didn't choose the job. Most people don't. And if you're like most of the caregivers I meet on house calls, you are very good at reporting on the person you care for — their sleep, their appetite, their medications, their moods — and very bad at reporting on yourself.

So here is the single most useful sentence I can give you.

At your next appointment — yours, not theirs — say these words to the doctor:

"I'm a caregiver, and I'm running on empty."

That one sentence starts more help than almost anything else you could say in an exam room.

Why this sentence works

Doctors are trained to respond to what's in front of them, and in a ten-minute appointment we respond to what you give us. When you spend your visit on your blood pressure and say nothing about the caregiving, the caregiving stays invisible — and invisible things don't get help.

The sentence works because it names the thing directly. It tells your doctor, in eight words, that there is a second patient in the room: the situation. It opens doors that stay closed otherwise — respite programs, home-care referrals, caregiver support groups, a check on your own sleep and mood, sometimes just a longer conversation than the one you booked.

You're allowed to be on the list

Here's what I tell families at the kitchen table: you are allowed to be on the list of people whose health matters. Not last on the list. On it.

Caregiver burnout isn't a character flaw and it isn't rare — it's the predictable result of doing a demanding job with no shifts, no weekends, and no one checking on the worker. The caregivers who last are not the ones who never need help. They're the ones who ask for it before they're desperate. That's not weakness; that's logistics.

This week

Book the appointment. Yours. If it feels indulgent, consider it maintenance on the one piece of equipment the whole operation depends on. And when you're in the room, say the sentence — even if your voice does something embarrassing halfway through. Doctors have heard voices do that before. It's usually the moment the visit starts mattering.
